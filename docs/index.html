Read the matrix · key takeaways

What the seven methods actually tell us

→

correlation is driven by gene expression level

Across every method tested, the same pattern repeats: highly expressed genes give clean, high correlations, while sparsely expressed genes give noisy, low correlations. If your gene of interest is detected in only a small fraction of cells, expect any correlation method to be unreliable until you boost the effective expression with pseudo-bulk or imputation.

→

the pattern generalises across plant species

The expression-driven reliability holds the same way in Arabidopsis, tomato, rice, soybean, maize, sorghum and Setaria. Whether you work in a model plant or a crop with limited annotation, the same rules of thumb apply. The decision tree in tab 05 is dataset-agnostic.

→

a high correlation value does not always mean the right answer

Some methods (CS-CORE, scCOSMIX) push correlation values close to 1 yet still fail to identify the true partner gene as the top correlate. Always check the rank of the gene of interest, not just the r value.

→

match the method to the data, not the other way around

Pearson on raw counts is a fast, reliable default for moderately or highly expressed genes. For very sparse pairs, switching from Pearson to Spearman or Kendall recovers the native gene from rank 163 to rank 5 (SCR sample). CS-CORE is for within-cell-type analyses; hdWGCNA is for gene modules. Pick the tool that fits your data regime and your goal.

→

fix the data before changing the method

If the same gene pair gives weak correlation across every method, the data is the bottleneck, not the method. Aggregate cells by cell type (pseudo-bulk, tab 03) or impute dropouts with a VAE (tab 04) before correlating again. These steps recover signal that no correlation method can pull out of unimproved raw counts.

How we extended the ground truth to every gene

Two halves of the same gene, a gold-standard simulation

The four promoter-reporter lines give us an engineered ground truth: the reporter and the native gene are driven by the same promoter, so they should correlate strongly. To extend this idea to every gene in the dataset, we simulate "two halves of the same gene" by count splitting (Neufeld et al., 2024). Each UMI count is randomly split in two, giving two count vectors that came from the same gene. They are, by construction, the same gene measured twice. We then ask whether each correlation method recovers the expected near-perfect correlation between the two halves.

Expected outcome: the two halves of a gene should correlate at r ≈ 1 with each other and rank as each other's top match, since they are literally the same gene. What actually happens: when a gene is expressed at very low levels, each half is dominated by sampling noise, so even two halves of the same gene fail to correlate well. The correlation is not broken; gene expression is just too sparse to support it.

Figure 3E · before vs after pseudo-bulk

Aggregating cells by cluster lifts the sparse pair

Reporter-to-native Pearson correlation in single-cell space versus after pseudo-bulk aggregation by cell-type cluster. SCR is the dominant beneficiary: its Pearson r climbs from 0.30 (raw single-cell) to 0.94 with cell-type pseudo-bulk. The other three lines already had strong signal at single-cell resolution and gain little.

Figure 3E vs 3F · clustering choice matters

Default Seurat clusters vs OMG cell-type clusters

Pseudo-bulk performance depends on how cells are grouped. The Seurat default (unsupervised Louvain at resolution 0.5) and OMG (ortholog-marker-group cell-type labels) give different pseudo-bulk correlations. OMG generally produces cleaner co-expression because the aggregation respects biological cell identity rather than transcriptomic similarity.

When to use pseudo-bulk · paper recommendation

Use pseudo-bulk ONLY when

Aggregating by cell type is justified only when your gene of interest is undetected in over 90% of cells and carries fewer than 2 UMI per positive cell. Otherwise the single-cell correlations are already informative and pseudo-bulk only sacrifices resolution.

Pseudo-bulk turns a single-cell measurement into a bulk-resolution one. If you need to keep single-cell resolution but still rescue a sparse pair, see the imputation tab instead.

Figure 4D · the headline result

VAE rescues the sparse case

The sparsest line, SCR, had a Pearson correlation of just 0.30 in raw data. After VAE imputation it climbs to 0.96, without inflating raw expression levels. AE and VAE gave the most consistent gains across all four lines.
